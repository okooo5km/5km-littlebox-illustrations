# Composition Methods

Choose one composition family per image. Do not combine too many.

## Balance Before Sequence

Build the image around a visual center of gravity, not a default left-to-right pipeline. Distribute meaningful mass into 3-5 zones across both axes. Balance one large form with two or three smaller counterweights; vary height, depth, scale, and orientation. Empty space should separate semantic groups, not merely occupy a quota.

Default to one of these non-linear structures:

- **Orbit**: Littlebox near the visual center with evidence, risks, options, or consequences arranged around it at varied radii and heights.
- **Diagonal tension**: a heavy cluster in one corner, Littlebox acting near center, and a smaller counterweight in the opposite corner.
- **Triangular balance**: Littlebox and two important object groups form a loose triangle instead of a row.
- **Staggered clusters**: two or three related groups sit at different heights and depths, connected by gesture rather than a rigid line.
- **Field composition**: repeated fragments occupy the canvas as a loose field while Littlebox creates a clear local order inside it.
- **Asymmetric counterweight**: Littlebox sits off-center and is balanced by a denser but smaller object cluster across the canvas.

Use a linear sequence only when time or causality is itself the concept. Even then, curve the path, stagger stages vertically, vary scale, and keep the result from reading as a process-chart row.

## Intake And Packaging

Best for raw notes, messy content, idea capture, research input, and knowledge organization.

Draw closed Littlebox off-center using twig arms to gather loose slips, blocks, or signals from a loose field into an external stack, sealed bundle, or labeled packet. Place input fragments at varied heights and use the output bundle as a counterweight. The lid stays closed.

## Before And After

Best for chaos-to-structure, manual-to-system, vague-to-clear, scattered-to-packaged.

Use contrasting zones rather than a rigid left/right row: loose material can rise diagonally behind Littlebox while the compact result sits lower in the opposite quadrant. Closed Littlebox performs the transformation with arms, stamps, straps, paths, or a separate press/slot prop. Do not open the box.

## Handoff Path

Best for workflows, customer journeys, publishing pipelines, sales handoffs, review loops, and assistant workflows.

Use one curved or looping amber path. Littlebox carries or seals the object at the key handoff point. Offset the endpoints vertically and balance them with small contextual props. Avoid straight-line process charts.

## Hidden Interior

Best for black boxes, model behavior, assumptions, hidden costs, and behind-the-scenes systems.

Littlebox stays closed. Show hidden behavior as small exterior slips, tags, peephole labels, or a few objects orbiting the sealed box. Do not draw a cutaway or visible interior.

## Pressure And Overflow

Best for bottlenecks, too many tools, overloaded context, queue problems, attention debt, or bad systems.

Littlebox stays closed while holding, bracing, tying, blocking, or carrying an external pile. Use coral only for the risk point. Do not show the lid opening under pressure.

## Evidence Bundle

Best for trust, proof, documentation, decision records, and validation.

Littlebox gathers small evidence slips with side-seam twig arms and seals them into one usable external package. Coral can be a final stamp.

## Mini Comic

Best for failure-to-fix sequences and humorous operational moments.

Use 2-3 loose panels maximum. Each panel shows one action. Keep Littlebox consistent. Do not make a comic strip unless the user's request naturally calls for sequential time.

## Metaphor Invention Rule

Convert the abstract idea into a physical box action:

- scattered inputs -> gather into an external stack beside closed Littlebox
- unclear decision -> sort pieces into labeled piles with twig arms
- fragile trust -> seal an evidence package
- broken handoff -> a sealed package snags on the path
- over-automation -> an external pile of tiny tools swamps closed Littlebox
- context compression -> Littlebox folds a large sheet with its arms into a small parcel
- publishing -> Littlebox stamps or hands off one clean signal

Then add one low-tech object only if it clarifies the idea: slip, tape, stamp, shelf, path, slot, scale, tag, latch, handle, or tiny stack.

## Anti-Repeat Rule

Do not reuse the same box action for every topic. Vary closed-box actions: sealing, stamping, carrying, sorting external slips, tying a bundle, blocking a path, handing off a parcel, folding a sheet, or bracing a pile.

## Spatial Rule

Distribute visual mass across both axes and at least three compositional zones. A wide image should not place everything on one horizontal line. Use one clear primary action plus supporting groups at different heights, scales, and depths. Check the thumbnail silhouette: neither half of the canvas should feel accidentally empty or overloaded.

## IP Consistency Rule

Composition may stretch Littlebox's action, not its identity. Keep the canonical front-left three-quarter box readable in every composition. If an action requires a different physical role, change the surrounding object first before deforming Littlebox. Littlebox can hold, push, seal, sort, carry, bridge, or brace an external overflow with two small side-seam twig arms; it should not become a random crate, tray, bag, scroll, machine, or anonymous package.

## Flow Mark Rule

- Use amber `#F59E0B` for flow paths, motion curves, and arrows.
- Flow marks should be thinner or lighter than Littlebox's black outline.
- Do not use large black arrows for movement; black arrows compete with the character and make the image feel like a diagram.
- Keep flow paths separate from the amber seam tape so they do not read as extra tape.
- Prefer one curved path over multiple straight arrows.
